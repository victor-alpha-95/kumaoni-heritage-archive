Proverbs, Riddles & Word-Play

आँण-कहावत
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-18 · review 2027-07

In two lines

A Kumaoni riddle is an आँण (aan) — "eat the pestle's worm or the cupboard's worm?" (answer: honey) — and the proverbs are the hills' compressed philosophy: "even a crow of one's own land is beloved" A. Printed collections exist (Dr. Saraswati Kohli's कुमाऊँनी कहावतें एवं मुहावरे among them); the living stock sits in grandparents' speech A.

What it is

Riddles (आँण/अहण). The genre name is attested by two independent sources, one citing the print magazine Kumgarh A. Attested examples with answers: the honey riddle above; "three mouths, ten feet" (bullock and ploughman); "climbs its own tree, leaves its foot in the ground" (a shoe); a drum riddle (the damua) A.

Proverbs. No distinct native word surfaced — sources use कहावत/लोकोक्ति; the finding itself is recorded rather than papered over [A — negative]. Attested examples: स्यापाक् ब्या में बिरालु बामन — "a cat as the Brahmin at a snake's wedding" (absurd talk); आपण देशो क कौव लै लाड़ौ — "even one's own land's crow is dear"; बाँजै लाकड़ केड़ी भलि… — worth over looks; सौन मरि सासु भदौ आय आँसु — "mother-in-law died in Sawan, the tears came in Bhado" (too-late feeling); the buck-passing chain of mother-in-law → daughter-in-law → dog → tail A.

Lullabies. Honesty note: no distinct Kumaoni lullaby genre-term could be verified; nyoli is a longing-song, not a lullaby, and the term "हिलजातर" returned nothing — an elder question, not a web question [U — gap].

Do this at home

  • Riddle night: ask the honey riddle at dinner; then ask the elders for one you've never heard — every unrecorded aan is collection work.
  • Adopt one proverb per month into family speech; the crow proverb travels especially well in the diaspora.
  • Check the book: Dr. Saraswati Kohli's collection, if a relative owns it, is exactly the print source this entry lacks — photograph the title page.
