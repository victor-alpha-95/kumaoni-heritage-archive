The Chand Dynasty: Champawat to Almora

चंद राजवंश
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-12 · review 2027-07

In two lines

For most of a millennium the Chands ruled Kumaon — first from Champawat, then from Almora, the town they founded. Under Rudra Chand the scattered hills were reunited under one name, Kumaon; under Baz Bahadur the kingdom treated with Shah Jahan's Delhi as a power in its own right.

What it is

Founding (contested). Tradition says Som Chand, an immigrant from Jhusi (near Prayagraj) related to the Kannauj royal house, married the daughter of the last Katyuri king Brahma Deva and received the Champawat garrison in dowry [A: S6, S4, S15 — dowry detail Hindi-source only]. When he founded the dynasty is one of Kumaoni history's genuinely open questions: sources claim 685 CE, 700, 953 (the official district history's pick), 1019–21, and 1027. The strongest scholarly treatment — Krishna Pal Singh (1973, Proc. Indian History Congress), cross-checking copper plates and the 1223 Baleshwar inscription — argues for the early 11th century, noting the traditional genealogies were compiled only in the mid-19th century [V for the existence of the dispute; Singh's dating recorded as the best-evidenced position: S3, S6, S4].

Champawat, then Almora. The early capital was Champawat with its Rajbunga fort and the Baleshwar temple complex. In 1560–63 the capital moved to a new town on Almora ridge — Alam Nagar / Rajapur (a name still found on copper plates). Sources split credit: Bhishma Chand laying the foundation (1560), Balo Kalyan Chand completing the move — the two-step version matches the dynasty's own reign-tables [recorded: S6, S15].

The dynasty at its height. - Rudra Chand met Akbar at Lahore (1587); the Akbarnama calls him "one of the great landlords of India," and the Ain-i-Akbari records a "Sarkar of Kumaon" of 21 mahals [V — named primary Mughal chronicle citations: S6]. Around 1581 the region was reunified "as Kumaon" under his rule [A: S7]. - Baz Bahadur Chand (1638–78) allied with Shah Jahan against Garhwal (1655), took the Terai and Dehradun, later instituted a poll tax remitted to Delhi; he built the Golu Devta temple at Ghorakhal — in this telling, for a general named Golu who died in his wars [A: S6 — contradicts the Katyuri-prince account; both recorded]. - Recurring wars with Garhwal ran for two centuries, largely inconclusive: Rudra Chand repulsed (1581), Jagat Chand briefly took Srinagar (1708–20), Garhwal recovered under Pradip Shah [A: S6]. - A caution: popular accounts merge Garur Gyan Chand (1374–1419, who secured Bhabar-Terai from the Delhi Sultan) with the later Gyan Chand (1698–1708, who fought Fateh Shah of Garhwal) — two rulers, one name [flagged: S6].

Society under the Chands. The court settled immigrant lineages deliberately — e.g. the five Panchpurviya Rajput clans granted jagirs under Ratan Chand (1450–88) [A: S6]. Status was graded visibly: sacred-thread counts distinguished ranks (the specific "Khas 3-thread vs Rajput 6-thread" rule is thin-sourced U, but the dynasty's own title system attests graded threads [A: S6]). Modern scholarship reads hill society's history as gradual Rajputisation — Khasa communities acquiring Rajput markers over generations — versus Atkinson's colonial racial framing; both lenses are documented in the archive with dates attached [A: S17, S1].

Fall. In early 1790 the Gorkha army crossed the Kali through Gangoli and took Almora; the last king, Mahendra Chand, fled to the plains [V: S6, S7]. Eight centuries of Chand rule ended in a season — the story continues in the Gorkha entry.

Do this at home

  1. Walk (or street-view) Almora's Lala Bazar–Karkhana Pati ridge — the Chand capital's spine survives in the old town's stone-paved market. If you have Almora roots, ask which mohalla your family lived in; mohalla names carry Chand-era occupational history.
  2. Retell the founding bargain: a Katyuri princess, a dowry of one fort at Champawat, and a dynasty that outlasted the Mughals it dined with.
  3. At Ghorakhal or Chitai, read the petition-letters to Golu Devta — then tell both versions of who Golu was (Katyuri prince / Chand general) and let the listener pick. Holding two versions honestly is the Kumaoni historical method.

Variations & open questions

  • Founding date: 685 / 700 / 953 / 1019–21 / 1027 CE — recorded, with Singh (1973) as best-evidenced; district portal says 953 [unresolved].
  • Almora founding credit: one-step (Kalyan Chand 1563) vs two-step (Bhishma 1560 → Kalyan) [recorded].
  • Golu Devta origin: Chand general vs Katyuri prince [unresolved; see origins entry].
  • "Chand patronage created a Kumaoni school of Pahari painting" — tier-3 only; Britannica/mainstream accounts of Pahari painting do not name a Kumaon school; likely conflation with Garhwal painting [U → gap register].
  • Gyan Chand conflation (1374 vs 1698 rulers) — flag when citing.
  • Thread-count caste rules under the Chands — needs a named primary source → gap register.
