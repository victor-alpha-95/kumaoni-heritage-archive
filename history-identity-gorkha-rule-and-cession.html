Gorkhyani: Gorkha Rule & the Coming of the British

गोरख्यानी (1790–1815)
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-12 · review 2027-07

In two lines

For twenty-five years (1790–1815) Kumaon was ruled from Kathmandu — a period so harsh that Gorkhyani survives in the language as a byword for oppression. It ended at Almora in April 1815, when Kumaon passed to the British under a local surrender convention, months before the Treaty of Sugauli made it official.

What it is

The conquest (1790). Invited by the disaffected Kumaoni minister-strongman Harsh Dev Joshi, Gorkha forces under Amar Singh Thapa crossed the Kali in 1790, advanced through Sor and Gangoli, and took Almora with little opposition; the last Chand king fled to the plains [V: S1-lineage, S6-Chand entry sources]. (A named "Battle of Hawalbagh" circulates only in exam-prep aggregators — unconfirmed U.)

The rule, as remembered. Kumaon spent 25 years under Gorkha administration — against Garhwal's 12 (conquered only in 1803); popular summaries often wrongly apply the 12-year figure to both [V — Atkinson's own contemporary phrase "twenty five years past": S1]. Atkinson recorded the verdict of 1815: "Nothing could exceed the hatred which the tyranny and exactions of twenty five years past had created, and no sooner had British forces entered the hills than the inhabitants began to join our camp" [V — verbatim, tier 1: S1]. Historian Shekhar Pathak notes the very word "Gorkhyani" became synonymous with cruelty in Kumaoni speech [A: S2-journalism quoting named historian]. Specific Gorkha tax names (Manga Kar, Ghar Pichhi Kar, the Kushahi Kar on Brahmins from 1797) are blog-sourced pending verification against Atkinson/Pradhan [U/A]. The forced-labour precedent of this era fed directly into the later Coolie Begar system [A: S3 citing Pathak 1991, peer-reviewed].

The war and the cession (1814–16). In the Anglo-Nepalese War, Kumaon fell fast — Gorkha ranks here were thinned by desertion, "the universal disaffection of the people" working against Kathmandu [V: S1]. Almora's final assault began 25 April 1815; the town fell around the 26th; and on 27 April 1815 the Convention of Almora was signed by Edward Gardner, Bam Shah, Chamu Bhandari and Jamadan Thapa — the instrument by which Kumaon actually changed hands [V — primary archival correspondence quoted: S1]. The Treaty of Sugauli (drafted December 1815, ratified 4 March 1816) later formalized Nepal's overall losses; saying Kumaon was "ceded at Sugauli" is the common imprecision this entry corrects [V: S1, S6-Sugauli].

What came immediately after. Lord Moira confirmed Edward Gardner as first Commissioner of Kumaon (his cousin Lt-Col William Gardner had commanded troops — two men, often conflated); in 1816 the young G.W. Traill took over and governed for twenty years [V: S1]. The frontier of Kumaon settled on the Kali — which is why the river remains, to this day, the boundary between Kumaoni and Nepali worlds that share the Mahakali's two banks, kinship, and the Doti-Kumaon historical braid.

Do this at home

  1. Ask an elder about "Gorkhyani" — whether they've heard it used for hard times generally. If yes, record the sentence; that's a living linguistic fossil of 1790–1815 and exactly what this archive needs (submit it!).
  2. Retell the hinge: one April week in 1815 — assault on the 25th, fall on the 26th, signatures on the 27th — decided the next 132 years. History often turns on days, not centuries.
