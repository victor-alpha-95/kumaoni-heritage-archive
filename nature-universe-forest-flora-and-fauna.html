The Forest: Flora & Fauna of Kumaon

बण-जंगल
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-12 · review 2027-07

In two lines

Kumaon's culture is written in two trees: the broadleaf banj oak that holds the water and feeds the cattle, and the colonial-favored chir pine that spread with the railways — a quarrel that burned through the 1916–21 forest protests and produced the world-notable Van Panchayat village forest councils of 1931 V. And it is sung in species: the kafal pako cuckoo, the ghughuti dove of the migration songs, the burans that opens spring [V/A].

What it is

Oak versus pine. Banj oak (c. 1500–2300 m) anchors water tables and fodder; chir pine (c. 900–1800 m) spread massively under colonial forestry for resin and railway sleepers from the 1860s onward V. Resentment of reserved forests ignited the 1916–1921 Kumaon agitation — chir plantations burned across Almora — forcing the Kumaon Forest Grievances Committee (1921) and then the Van Panchayat Rules of 1931: community-managed village forests, a system still running today with counts reported between ~11,200 and ~12,167 (the Uttarakhand Economic Survey figure; range recorded, not resolved) [V/A]. Chipko honesty: the famous 1973–74 tree-hugging is Garhwal's story (Mandal, Reni, Gaura Devi); Kumaon's forest politics ran through its own earlier protests and later merged into the statehood movement [V — attribution kept straight].

Trees of the culture (each verified): kafal (Myrica esculenta) — the summer fruit whose folklore says a wrongly beaten daughter became the bird crying kafal pako, mil ni chakho; the caller is the Indian cuckoo per ethno-ornithology, though one telling paints a magpie — conflict recorded A. Burans (Rhododendron arboreum), the state tree, its petals gathered by children for Phool Dei V. Deodar, the "wood of the gods," ringing Jageshwar's shrines [V — grove; U — Purana specifics]. Bhimal for fodder and rope-fibre V; ringal for GI-tagged basketry V; timur (Zanthoxylum armatum), collected and traded by the Bhotiya of Pithoragarh [V — peer-reviewed]; utis alder pollarded on terraces [V — ICRAF]; hisalu, the golden raspberry praised by the poet Gumaani Pant A; kilmora, ghingaru [A/U]; pinalu taro, whose prized gaderi comes from Lobanj, Bageshwar [V — peer-reviewed].

Animals of the story. The Champawat tigress — a tiger, ~436 deaths, shot by Jim Corbett in 1907 — and the Panar leopard (Almora, ~400 deaths, 1910) are Kumaon's man-eaters; the more famous Rudraprayag leopard is Garhwal's, a standard conflation this archive refuses V. Leopard conflict is present-tense: ~234 deaths statewide 2011–2021, with behaviour-change programs measurably reducing attacks V. The ghughuti dove (Streptopelia) carries the ache of the married daughter's longing in Kumaon's best-loved songs [V — peer-reviewed folklore study]; the monal is the state bird V; monkey crop-raiding is now itself a driver of field abandonment V.

Protected geography. Corbett NP (1936, first national park of mainland Asia) V; Binsar WLS (1988, created for the oak) V; Askot Musk Deer Sanctuary (1986, Pithoragarh) V; Nanda Devi Biosphere Reserve — shared, majority Garhwal [V — flagged shared].

Do this at home

  • Learn the two trees: on any hill drive, teach a child to tell banj from chir — and why the difference decided a century of protest.
  • Taste the calendar: kafal in May–June, hisalu after it, burans juice in spring — ask which the family picked as children, and where.
  • Harela 2027: plant the sapling where it can live; the festival's modern plantation drives are the culture's own conservation instrument.
  • Retell one story: the kafal-pako girl, or the Champawat tigress — both are Kumaon's own.
