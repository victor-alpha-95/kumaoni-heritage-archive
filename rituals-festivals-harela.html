Harela

हरेला
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verification📅 2026-07-16 (sankranti 23:44 IST; some calendars & the state holiday list observe 2026-07-17 — divergence recorded, not resolved)Updated 2026-07-12 · review 2027-05

In two lines

Harela ("the day of green") opens Shravan in Kumaon: ten days earlier every household sows seven grains in a basket of soil, and on the day the elders cut the young shoots and place them on each family member's head with the blessing jī raye, jāgi raye — live on, stay awake. It is a harvest prayer, a Shiva–Parvati festival, and now Uttarakhand's tree-planting day, all at once.

What it is

Harela is the most important folk festival of the Kumaon region V, marking the start of the month of Shravan at Karka Sankranti (the sun's entry into Cancer), the onset of the monsoon sowing cycle V. The word means "day of green" in Kumaoni V. It carries three intertwined meanings: an agricultural omen ritual (the sown harela shoots forecast the coming crop — the lusher the shoots, the better the harvest) V; a commemoration of the union of Shiva and Parvati, worshipped through household clay idols (dikāre) V; and, in recent decades, an environmental festival of mass tree-planting promoted by the state V.

Harela technically occurs three times a year — in Chaitra, Shravan, and Ashwin — but the Shravan Harela is the socially paramount one; the other two are now minor or fading [A: S3-HI].

Since 2020 Harela has been a public holiday in Uttarakhand [A: S9, S11]. In 2026 it falls on Thursday 16 July, with the sankranti moment at 23:44 IST [V: S1-HI, S9]; because the transit falls near midnight, some calendars and reportedly the state holiday list observe 17 July [A: S12] — both dates are recorded here per charter §6.

How it is practised

Sowing (9–11 days before). The household sows five, seven, or (some families) eleven kinds of seed — lists vary by source and family: wheat, barley, paddy/maize, gahat (horse gram), bhatt (black soybean), urad, mustard, chana appear across lists [V for the practice; the exact list is a recorded divergence]. Soil and seed are layered four to five times in a small container — commonly a ringāl (dwarf-bamboo) basket, in some accounts decorated with aipan [A: S4-HI] — kept in the home temple, in the dark (which keeps the shoots pale yellow-green), and watered morning and evening, in some accounts with water from a naulā or dhārā (spring) [A: S1-HI, S4-HI]. Sowing-day divergence is explicit in sources: 9, 10, or 11 days before, "determined by each family's own tradition" [V: S1-HI, S4-HI]. For 2026 that meant sowing around 6–8 July [A: S1-HI].

Dikāre (eve of Harela). Households — often women and children — hand-shape unfired idols of the Shiva–Parvati family (Ganesh and others depending on the maker's skill) from red clay kneaded with cotton, coated with rice-flour paste and painted with natural pigments; convention colours Shiva blue and Parvati white [V: S4-HI, S6]. A damru, trishul, moon, mouse, and Nandi may be modelled alongside [A: S6]. The idols are placed among the harela shoots and worshipped on the eve — dikāre pūjan [V: S3-HI, S13].

Cutting and blessing (Harela day). The head of the family cuts the shoots and offers them to the deities [A: S4-HI]. Elders then bless each junior, touching the harela sprigs to feet, knees, shoulders, and finally placing them on the head [V: S3-HI, S14], while reciting the blessing verse (below). Men wear harela in their caps or behind the ear, women in the hair; sprigs are fixed to doorways with cow dung [A: S4-HI]. Blessing runs from eldest to youngest [A — implied across sources, not explicitly stated; flagged].

The blessing verse. Two published variants, recorded side by side (oral tradition legitimately varies; transcriptions unverified against print — see gaps):

Variant A [A: S3-HI]:

जी रये, जागि रये, तिष्टिये, पनपिये, दुब जस हरी जड़ हो, ब्यर जस फइये, हिमाल में ह्यूं छन तक, गंग ज्यू में पांणि छन तक, यो दिन और यो मास भेटनैं रये, अगासाक चार उकाव, धरती चार चकाव है जये, स्याव कस बुद्धि हो, स्यू जस पराण हो।

Variant B [A: S1-HI]:

जी रये, जागि रये। यो दिन-यो बार कैं भ्यटनै रये। तेरी दुबकी जस जड़, पाती क जस पौव है जौ। स्याव जस बुद्धि, स्युं जस तराण ऐ जौ। हिमाल में ह्युं छन तक, गंगज्यू में पाणि छन तक। तू जी रये, जागि रए।

Gist (working gloss, moderate confidence): Live on, stay awake. May you meet this day and this month again and again. Roots like dūb grass, growth like the fields. As long as there is snow on the Himalaya, as long as there is water in the Ganga. Wit like the syāv (fox/jackal), strength/long life like the syū. Individual word glosses (ब्यर, स्यू) are uncertain — logged as a gap.

Food. After the puja, families eat puri, bada/bara (urad fritters), kheer, and halwa [V: S1-HI, S6]; offerings to the deities include fruit, puri, kheer, and a jaggery-flour cheelā [A: S1-HI]. Interview-sourced additions: puye (fermented semolina-cream fritters) and singal (semolina spiral, jalebi-like) [A: S6 — named residents of Almora/Bhowali/Dehradun]. Claims that bal mithai/singori are specific to Harela are blog-only U.

Modern evolution. Public holiday since 2020 [A: S9]. The state runs Harela Mahotsav plantation campaigns; in 2025 over 8.13 lakh saplings were reported planted in one day, aligned with the national "Ek Ped Maa Ke Naam" drive [A: S10]. Schools encourage sapling planting [A: S2]. "Save the environment" has become the festival's public slogan [A: S2].

Do this at home (this week — festival is 16/17 July)

  1. Say it. On Harela morning, the eldest present places any green sprigs (dūb grass, or even a houseplant cutting) on each family member's head — feet, knees, shoulders, head — saying "जी रये, जागि रये". Call or video-call grandparents and ask them to give you the blessing; ask which verse they say — and write it down (that's community verification).
  2. Plant one thing. A sapling if you have ground; a herb pot on a windowsill if you don't. That is the modern Harela.
  3. Cook one dish. Kheer or urad bada — both feasible in any city kitchen.
  4. Next year, do it fully: around 6–8 days into July, sow 5–7 kinds of whole grains/pulses from the kitchen (wheat, mustard, urad…) in layered soil in any pot, keep it in a dark corner, water twice daily, and cut on Harela day.

Variations & open questions

  • Sowing lead time: 9 vs 10 vs 11 days — family-level divergence, documented as such V.
  • Seed list and count (5/7/11) varies by source and community V.
  • 2026 observance date: 16 July (sankranti day) vs 17 July (holiday list) [recorded, unresolved].
  • Garhwal celebrates the same sankranti differently — as Mol Sankranti / Rai-Sagrān; there, reportedly only thread-initiated men sow the barley [A: S3-HI]. Kept distinct per the Garhwal-confusion caution; NOT merged into Kumaoni practice.
  • Blessing-verse word glosses (ब्यर, स्यू) uncertain; verse text not yet checked against a printed/scholarly source.
  • District-level variation within Kumaon (Almora vs Pithoragarh vs Champawat): undocumented online → gap register.
  • Contemporary prevalence of dikāre-making: no quantitative evidence → gap register.
  • Whether songs sung at Harela are correctly called "jagar": one blog claim, likely conflation with the jagar invocation ritual [U — excluded, flagged].
