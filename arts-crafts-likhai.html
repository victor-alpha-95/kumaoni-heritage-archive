Likhai: The Carved House

लिखाई
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-18 · review 2027-07

In two lines

Likhai — "writing" — is the woodcarving that turned Kumaoni facades into pages: Ganesha over nearly every carved kholi doorway, vines of Lakshmi's lotus down the jambs, named window-forms (tyapari, two- and three-panelled) [V — incl. peer-reviewed fieldwork]. Its master generation is gone — The Hindu profiled Ganga Ram as "the last surviving master craftsman" before his death in 2018 — but Munsiari's Ordh carvers and an NMHS-funded training revival keep the chisel moving [V/A].

What it is

The grammar. Kholi = the carved ceremonial doorway (the term one institutional source labels Garhwali but every source uses; "daroj" appears in no source beyond the one academic architecture paper — the naming conflict now leans kholi, recorded) [A — updated]. Ganesha top-center; lotus/vine = Lakshmi; geometric panels a later layer; Munsiari adds Tibetan-influenced dragons and arch-forms from the border trade [V — fieldwork]. Windows: tyapari, dodyari/teen-dyari A.

The carvers. Caste attribution genuinely conflicts: tourism sources say "Oar"/Shilpkar; academic fieldwork (20-village Munsiari survey) names the historical carving caste as Dom, later folded into the Shilpkar category — both recorded, likely historical-name vs modern-category [A — conflict held]. Spread: all six hill districts of Kumaon, while Garhwal's carving concentrates in temples rather than houses A.

Decline & revival. The causal chain is documented: colonial timber extraction → forest-law limits → timber costs → brick/RCC — down to owners molding cement to imitate carving [V — fieldwork]. Almora's bazaar lanes keep facades above the shopfronts A. Revival: National Mission on Himalayan Studies-funded training via Himal Prakriti (Munsiari) A; a national handicrafts-catalogue listing exists, but GI status is not confirmed against the registry — recorded as unverified, unlike Aipan/Ringal/Tamta whose registrations are primary-verified [A/U — discipline kept].

Do this at home

  • Photograph the kholi: if the ancestral house stands, its doorway is the family's most endangered artwork — shoot it straight-on and in raking light, note the motifs, send it in.
  • Read one facade in Almora's bazaar: find the Ganesha, then the vine — once seen, never unseen.
  • Ask who carved it: family memory of the carpenter's name/community is exactly what the caste-attribution conflict needs.
