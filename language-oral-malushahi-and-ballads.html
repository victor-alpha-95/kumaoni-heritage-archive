Malushahi-Rajula & the Oral Epics

मालूशाही-राजुला
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-18 · review 2027-07

In two lines

Malushahi-Rajula is Kumaon's great sung romance — a Katyuri prince, a Shauka trader's daughter, poisoning, magical resurrection, and (depending on the singer) reunion or death — performed through the night by hurkiya bards [V — academic]. One bard, Gopi Das, sang 2,200 lines that became a three-volume German scholarly edition; another, Mohan Singh "Reethagadi" Bora, became guru to the scholar-composer Mohan Upreti, who published the ballad through the Sangeet Natak Akademi V.

What it is

The story. Malushahi, Katyuri prince of Bairat (son of Dulashahi); Rajula of the Shauka (Bhotiya) trading world of Malli Darma — betrothal, obstruction, Rajula's journey, Malushahi's poisoning in disguise, revival by magic, and two attested endings (reunion vs death), because the epic lives in versions, not a text [V — Sahapedia + Asian Ethnology independently].

The performance. Sung by hurkiya bards — for the Malushahi specifically, scholarship also records the performer-pair terms Das (singer) and Dholi (drummer) [V — HIMALAYA journal]. No ritual preface is required (it is entertainment-epic, not jagar); weddings, fairs and winter gatherings were its stages A. The documentation: Konrad Meissner's Mālushāhī and Rājulā (Harrassowitz, 1985; text, translation, commentary, glossary of Gopi Das's performance) V; Mohan Upreti's Malushahi: The Ballad of Kumaon (Sangeet Natak Akademi, 1980), documenting three performers' versions across caste lines V; the "Rithagarh Episode" — how Upreti found his guru — published in Sangeet Natak and hosted by the Ministry of Culture V.

The rest of the ballad-shelf, honestly. Heroic bhadauli ballads of the Katyuri kings are attested only by Sahapedia — single-sourced [A/U]. "Ramaul" as an independent epic is unresolved: one source treats it as a genre, another as a character within Malushahi — recorded, not asserted U. The jagar ballads of Golu and Gangnath belong to the ritual-landscape entry.

Do this at home

  • Tell it in five minutes at the table — prince, trader's daughter, poison, revival — then say: "and every singer ends it differently." That sentence is oral tradition.
  • Listen to one sung passage (recordings exist on YouTube's folk channels — Resources page); notice the hurka's gallop under the verse.
  • Ask elders whether a Das ever sang at a family wedding — living memory of performance is now the scarcest source.

Variations & open questions

Two endings [V — recorded as legitimate variance]. Ramaul U. Bhadauli corroboration U. Whether any recording of Reethagadi survives: not found U. Shauka community's own telling of Rajula — community-phase priority [—].
