Water & Sacred Groves

नौल-धार · देव वन
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-18 · review 2027-07

In two lines

A naula is a covered stone stair-tank built over a spring, sacred enough that a bride prays there first and a red guardian-snake is said to punish neglect; Dwarahat had ~360 of them a century ago and keeps ~36 in perennial flow V. The groves that shelter Kumaon's springs and temples — Jageshwar's deodar, Gangolihat's Darukavan — are the same conservation instrument in tree form V.

What it is

The architecture of water. Naula: a tank walled on three sides with a stepped approach on the fourth, roofed, spring-fed [V — T1]. Dhara: a flowing spout, typed by posture — sirpatia (drink standing), murpatia (bending), patveedia (seasonal) [V — T1]. Simar/gajar: marshy groundwater tracts turned to paddy [A — single source]. Champawat's Ek Hathiya Naula ("one-handed" — built overnight by a single-handed craftsman, though the legend splits: fearful "Jagannath" who fled vs vengeful "Rangnath" whose hand a king had cut off) and Baleshwar Naula are the famous ones; Syunrakot Naula (Almora, Chand-era, 14th–15th c.) became a nationally Protected Monument in January 2023 V.

The decline and the fight-back. Roughly half the Himalayas' perennial springs have dried or gone seasonal (NITI Aayog 2018); Almora town, once 300+ naulas, and Dwarahat, once 360, are much diminished V. Revival is real and named: CHIRAG restored 716 springs in Kumaon (2008–2019); the Naula Foundation (Bishan Singh Baneshi, Ranikhet, 2018 — founded after he lacked water for his mother's last rites) has revived 150+ V.

The sacred groves. Jageshwar: ~125 stone temples in a deodar forest at ~1,870 m, 7th–8th c. [V — state tourism primary]. Haat Kalika (Gangolihat) sits in deodar the tradition calls Darukavan A. A peer-reviewed Western-Himalaya study ties grove protection to biodiversity; named grove-deities include Haat Kaali, Kotgyari Devi, Hariyali Devi [V/A]. Correction: "Chiplakot" is a high alpine range and its deity, not a deodar temple-grove — retired from this category [V — negative].

Do this at home

  • Adopt your naula: find the family spring, learn its name and type, ask who cleans it — and photograph its carved stone. Neglect is the whole crisis; attention is the whole fix.
  • The bride's prayer: if there's a wedding, keep the naula visit alive.
  • Support a reviver: CHIRAG and the Naula Foundation take volunteers and donations — a diaspora contribution that lands exactly where the culture's water does.
