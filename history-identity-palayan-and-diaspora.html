Palayan: Migration & the Kumaoni Diaspora

पलायन
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-12 · review 2027-04

In two lines

The 2011 Census counted over a thousand empty villages in Uttarakhand, and Almora district's population actually fell — the hills are emptying into Haldwani, Delhi, and beyond. Palayan is the reason this archive exists: culture transmitted for centuries by simply living in a village now has to be transmitted deliberately.

What it is

The scale. Of 16,793 census villages in 2011, 1,053 had no inhabitants and another 405 had fewer than ten people [V — peer-reviewed: S6]. Almora and Pauri Garhwal recorded absolute population decline over 2001–11 — near-unique in India — while plains districts grew 30%+ [V: S6 citing Mamgain & Reddy 2015]. Pauri is the worst-affected district and Almora the second, on both out-migration and (in 2020) return-migration counts [A: S11, ThePrint]. Later "ghost village" counts — 734 more depopulated since 2011 (Palayan Ayog 2018), 1,787 "fully abandoned" (later commission count), 3,946 "fully migrated" over a decade (Ayog district-report aggregation) — are not comparable with each other or with the Census: different years, definitions, and survey methods; this entry refuses a single headline number on principle [V for the non-comparability: S8, S10, S12].

The drivers. The Palayan Ayog's own breakdown: employment 52%, education 18%, healthcare 8%, declining agriculture 6%, wildlife crop damage 4%, basic infrastructure 3% [V: S8 — Down To Earth Hindi quoting commission report]. Most migrants don't go far: ~36% to other districts, ~29% out of state, under 1% abroad [A: S8, Tehri report]. The pattern has a name and a genealogy: the "money-order economy" (coined by R.S. Bora, 1996) — men remit, women and elders farm; remittances now cover an estimated 30–40% of hill household income [V for concept: S6; the 30–40% figure tier-3, U]. Its sharpest political trace: in 33 of 34 hill assembly seats, women out-vote men — because the men aren't there [V: S10, named analyst].

What it does to culture. Kumaoni is UNESCO-listed as vulnerable; a young diaspora that "has heard of Uttarakhand's culture but can't tell you what it means" is the recorded lament [A: S12]; craft knowledge like koti banal earthquake-resistant timber construction loses its carriers when villages empty [A: S12]. Census data undercounts the diaspora itself, since many Kumaonis outside the hills report Hindi as mother tongue — Delhi is the largest recorded Kumaoni-speaker hub outside the region (32,674 in 2011; the true ethnic figure is far larger) [V/A: S16]. Diaspora institutions exist but are thinly documented online — the Uttarayani Mela is replicated at Bareilly; a living Delhi "Kurmanchal Parishad" could not be substantiated in this sweep [recorded honestly: S17, gap]. COVID brought a documented reverse wave (interim: 59,360 returnees by April 2020; Pauri and Almora first) and a natural experiment in return [A: S-ayog-interim; V for the phenomenon: S6 — 63% returned for job loss, only half received any assistance]. The state's counter-programme (Mukhyamantri Palayan Roktham Yojna, targeting 474 worst-hit villages) exists; its effectiveness is, on the record so far, unproven — in 2015 the government could not name in the Assembly the six villages it claimed to have repopulated [V: S19, S9].

Why this entry closes the loop. Every other entry in this archive carries a "do this at home" hook because of the facts on this page: the courtyard, the festival crowd, and the family kitchen that once transmitted culture automatically now exist mostly in memory or in miniature, in flats in Noida and Almora bazaar alike. Documentation plus deliberate practice is the replacement pipeline. You, reading this, are the pipeline.

Do this at home

  1. Record your family's palayan story this week — three questions to an elder: Which village? Who left first, when, and why? Who still goes back, and for what? Ten minutes of audio. That recording is simultaneously family heirloom and primary source — submit it to the archive's community phase.
  2. Go back once a year with a job to do — a Harela sowing, a roof repair, the village temple's bhandara. Villages die of absence, not distance.
  3. If you're in the diaspora, host one Kumaoni evening a year (Uttarayani in January works): one dish, one song, one story — the portable village.
