The Kumaoni Language

कुमाऊँनी बोली
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-18 · review 2027-07

In two lines

Kumaoni is an Indo-Aryan (Central Pahari) language of ~2 million speakers, written in Devanagari, that Grierson's Linguistic Survey found split into "not less than twelve" dialects across the hill valleys V. UNESCO lists it as vulnerable; it has no place in school, office, or court — which is precisely why every phrase a family keeps using is an act of preservation [V/A].

What it is

Classification. Indo-Aryan → Northern Zone → Central Pahari, per modern reference linguistics (Glottolog, following Hendriksen) V. Grierson's 1916 Linguistic Survey of India named twelve dialects — Khasparjia, Phaldakotiya, Pachhai, Kumauni of Nainital, Bhabari of Rampur, Kumaiya, Chaugarkhiya, Gangoli, Danpuria, Soryali, Askoti, Sirali — commonly grouped four ways (Central/Kali, NE, SE, Western), with Central (Kali) Kumaoni of Almora, including Khasparjiya, as the prestige standard [V — T1]. Grierson's old "Darad-Khas substrate" theory is treated as superseded; the modern account traces the base to Sauraseni Prakrit with residual Khasa phonological features [A — recorded as scholarly evolution].

Status. ~2.0 million speakers (2011 Census; one academic source gives 2,081,057 — figures recorded, primary Census table check queued) A. UNESCO Atlas: vulnerable/unsafe (label variance recorded) A. Script: Devanagari (SIL ScriptSource); a claimed earlier Takri usage is attested only generically for Khasa-family languages, not Kumaoni specifically [V/U]. Eighth Schedule campaigns for constitutional recognition (with Garhwali) are real and ongoing — a 2010 private member's bill, postcard campaigns, and MHA's own list of pending demands, which also notes India has no fixed criteria for inclusion [V — MHA primary].

The literature. Gumani Pant (1790–1846; an 1770 birth-claim also circulates — conflict recorded) is regarded as the first Kumaoni poet A; Gauri Dutt Pandey "Gaurda" (1872–1939) made Holi songs carry social conscience A; Sher Singh Bisht "Anpadh" and Mathura Datt Mathpal (editor of the journal Dudboli) carry the modern line [A — thin sourcing]. The best structured learning resource found online remains speakkumaoni.com's audio lessons A.

Do this at home

  • Adopt the ten-phrase floor: greetings, kinship terms (see the Everyday Speech entry), and one blessing — used weekly at home, they outweigh any archive.
  • Locate your dialect: find your family's village on the twelve-dialect map above and ask an elder which words mark it ("how do WE say 'what'?"). Send the answer in.
  • One lesson: try a speakkumaoni.com audio lesson with a child.
