Folk Music & Its Instruments

लोक संगीत
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-18 · review 2027-07

In two lines

Kumaon's soundscape rests on the hurka — the hourglass drum without which no jagar can be held — carried by hereditary Hurkiya musicians of historically marginalized communities whose repertoire is the region's oral literature V. Around it: the shared pan-Uttarakhand dhol-damau, the battle-brass turi and ransingha, the colonial-import-turned-native masakbeen, and the song "Bedu Pako Baramasa," which Mohan and Naima Upreti carried from Almora to the nation [V/A].

What it is

The instruments. Hurka: wood body, goat-skin heads, the jagar's engine V. Dhol-damau: shared across Garhwal, Kumaon and Himachal — not Kumaon-exclusive, with the Dhol Sagar treatise tied academically to Garhwali drumming families [V — boundary recorded]. Turi, nagphani, ransingha: the brass battle-horns of processions A. Masakbeen: colonial Scottish bagpipe, assimilated (see chholiya entry) V. Binai (jaw harp): near-vanished, single-source U.

The musicians, documented respectfully. The hereditary players — Hurkiya, Bajgi, Das, Dholi, Auji — belong to historically "low-caste," economically marginal communities; academic sources document both their centrality (guardians of the epics, first invocation rights at sowing) and the modern attrition as stigma and DJ-band economics push the young out [V — T1 journals + Sahapedia]. This archive records their names as artists first.

The carriers to the nation. Mohan Upreti (1928–97): composer-director, "Bedu Pako Baramasa," founder of Parvatiya Kala Kendra (1968), documenter of Malushahi V. Naima Khan Upreti (1938–2018): sang the 1952 premiere and 1955 HMV recording of Bedu Pako; NSD-trained, Doordarshan producer, PKK's later president V. Girish Tiwari "Girda" (1945–2010): poet-singer of the movements — Chipko to statehood A. AIR Almora and the 1955–78 AIR field-recording program shaped and preserved the canon [A/V].

Do this at home

  • Learn Bedu Pako — one verse, everyone can; it is the lowest-friction piece of Kumaoni identity in existence (and the Kumaon Regiment marches to it).
  • At any jagar or wedding, find the hurka player, learn his name, and thank him by it — the archive's respect principle in one gesture.
  • Play the instruments game with children at a baraat: dhol or damau? turi or masakbeen?
