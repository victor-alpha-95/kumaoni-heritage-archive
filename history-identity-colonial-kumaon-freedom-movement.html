British Kumaon & the Freedom Movement

अंग्रेजी राज और कुली बेगार
attestedinternet-sourced, pending community verificationUpdated 2026-07-12 · review 2027-07

In two lines

On 14 January 1921, at the Uttarayani Mela in Bageshwar, thousands took Saryu water in hand and swore before Bagnath never to render forced labour again — and the coolie begar system collapsed. Gandhi called it a bloodless revolution; it remains the proudest single day in Kumaoni political memory.

What it is

An unusual colony. Kumaon was run as a "non-regulation" province — for roughly seventy years effectively by just three men: G.W. Traill (settlement surveys from 1817), J.H. Batten, and Sir Henry Ramsay, commissioner for 28 years and nicknamed the "King of Kumaon," a Kumaoni-speaking administrator who was nonetheless the same man who fined the peasants of Someshwar in 1878 for refusing begar — both facts belong in the record [V: S5, S2-Pathak]. Nainital was founded as a hill station in 1841 (Traill had likely seen the lake decades earlier) [A: S-Nainital]. From 1878, and sharply in 1911–16 (≈3,000 sq mi reserved), colonial forest law fenced villagers out of their own woods — the deepest grievance of the period [V: S4-Swarthmore/Guha].

Coolie begar — and 14 January 1921. Three linked extractions burdened every village: coolie begar (unpaid forced labour), coolie utar (nominally paid porterage), coolie burdayash (requisitioned provisions) [V — definitions per Pathak: S2]. Resistance built for decades — the Almora Akhbar editorialized against it in 1891 [V: S2] — and organized through the Kumaon Parishad (founded Nainital, 30 Sept 1916; sessions at Almora 1917, Haldwani 1918 — where Hargovind Pant's abolition resolution passed — Kotdwar 1919, Kashipur 1920) [V: S2, S-parishad]. At the Uttarayani Mela, Bageshwar, 14 January 1921, with the district's entire coercive apparatus amounting to "21 officials, 25 soldiers, and 500 bullets," Badri Datt Pandey and the assembled crowd took the Saryu-water oath before Bagnath temple to render begar no more; registers were seized and thrown into the river [V: S2 (Pathak), S3; oath wording is the stable popular formulation, A]. The district magistrate called the movement "revolutionary"; Gandhi reportedly called its effect "a bloodless revolution" in Young India [A — original issue not yet traced], and in 1929 at Almora credited Kumaonis with having abolished begar themselves [V: S2]. Pandey became "Kumaon Kesari", Hargovind Pant "Jannayak" [V: S3]. One honest caveat from Pathak: the artisanal Shilpkar castes, spurned by upper-caste society, largely stood apart and looked to the British for patronage — the movement's recorded weakness [V: S2].

Forests, fire, and the Van Panchayats. Forest satyagraha followed: organized incendiarism in 1916 and on mass scale in 1921 (recorded figures diverge — ~246,000 acres/395 fires vs ~82,880 ha/317 fires — both trace loosely to Guha; treat as order-of-magnitude) [A: S4]. The colonial response eventually conceded the Forest Council Rules of 1931, creating the Van Panchayat system of village-managed forests — still functioning today, roughly 3,000 councils [V: S4].

Gandhi in Kumaon; the last phase. Gandhi spent about ten days (some say two weeks) at Kausani in June 1929, writing his Anasakti Yoga commentary — the Anasakti Ashram marks it; separately, the Gandhian Sarla Behn (Catherine Mary Heilman) founded Lakshmi Ashram at Kausani in 1946, seeding a lineage of hill women activists [V: S-indianculture, S-SarlaBehn; the two ashrams are distinct]. In Quit India (1942), police firings at Deghat (Almora) and Salam/Dhamdho (25 Aug 1942) killed protesters whose names are kept locally — martyr lists vary across sources and need verification [A/U: S13-14]. And Kumaon gave the national movement Govind Ballabh Pant of Khoont village, Almora — later UP's first Chief Minister and Bharat Ratna [V: S-GBP]. (The "Kumaon Regiment, raised 1813" lineage is an administrative renaming of a Hyderabad-raised unit; the actually-Kumaoni battalion dates to Ranikhet, 1917 — commonly conflated [V-flag: S9].)

Do this at home

  1. Every 14 January, tell the Bageshwar story with the Ghughutia sweets: Kumaon's Uttarayani is a double anniversary — the winter festival and the day a crowd with river water in hand out-faced an empire holding 500 bullets. One sentence of it makes a child stand taller.
  2. Find your village's Van Panchayat (most Kumaoni villages have one) and ask who its sarpanch is — you'll be touching a living institution won by the 1921 generation.
  3. In Kausani, skip neither ashram: Anasakti (Gandhi, 1929) and Lakshmi (Sarla Behn, 1946) — ten minutes apart, two different revolutions.

  • Deghat/Salam 1942 martyr names: inconsistent across Hindi sources → verify locally (elder phase).
  • "Salam Satyagraha = Bardoli of Kumaon, named by Gandhi": exam-prep-only sourcing, chronologically shaky U → gap register.
  • Kumaon Parishad's 1926 merger into Congress: JSTOR paper paywalled — tier-1 upgrade path.
  • Speech vs oath: Pandey's archivally-recorded 12 Jan speech (forest grievances) and the 14 Jan oath are different moments of a multi-day event — never merge into one quote [recorded].
